Law of Logarithms

Logarithms are the inverse of exponentiation and just like exponents, they have a set of given laws. Laws of logarithms are rules that can be applied to simplify and solve complicated logarithmic equations.

Video Tutorial

Product Law

The product rule for logarithms can be used to simplify a logarithm of a product by rewriting it as a sum of individual logarithms. $$\log_{b}{uv}=\log_{b}{u}+\log_{b}{v}$$

Illustrative Example 1

Expand: $\log_{6}{24}$ Expand $24$ into factors that we can later simplify: $\log_{6}{(6\cdot 4)=\log_{6}{6}+\log_{6}{4}}$ Recall Property 2 of Logarithms: $1+\log_{6}{(4)}$ Further expand the argument of the second logarithm: $1+\log_{6}{(2^2)}$ Bring out the exponent using the Power Law of Logarithms: $ \boxed{ 1+2\log_{6}{(2)} } $

Recall: Property 2 of Logarithms

When the base and the argument of the logarithm are equal. The result will be $1$. $$log_{b}{b}=1$$

Quotient Law

The quotient rule for logarithms says that the logarithm of a quotient is equal to a difference of logarithms. Just as with the product rule, we can use the inverse property to derive the quotient rule. $$\log_{b}{\frac{u}{v}}=\log_{b}{u}-\log_{b}{b}$$

Illustrative Example 1

Expand: $ \log_{2}{(\frac{32}{8})} $ Expand the logarithm: $ \log_{2}{(\frac{32}{8})}=\log_{2}{32}+\log_{2}{8} $ Simplify: $ \log_{2}{(2^5)}+\log_{2}{(2^3)} $ We can now extract their exponent as per Property 3 of Logarithms: $ = 5-3 \newline \boxed{= 2} $

Recall: Property 3 of Logarithms

When the base of the logarithm and the base of the argument is the same. The logarithm is equal to the exponent of the argument's base. $$log_{b}{b^x}=x$$

Power Law

The power rule for logarithms can be used to simplify the logarithm of a power by rewriting it as the product of the exponent times the logarithm of the base. $$\log_{b}{u^n}=n\log_{b}{u}$$

Illustrative Example 1

Expand: $ \log_{10}{64} $ Simplify $64$ into $8^2$: $ \log_{10}{(8^2)} $ Bring out the exponent using the law: $ 2\log_{10}{(8)} $ Further expand the argument: $ 2\log_{10}{(2^3)} $ Bring out the exponent: $ = 3 \cdot 2 \log_{10}{(2)} \newline \boxed{= 6 \log_{10}{(2)}} $
